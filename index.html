Key Findings

Indiana's growth is structurally historic. The Fever's +319% attendance surge — from 4,067 to ~17,036 per game — is the largest single-season attendance jump in WNBA history by a wide margin. Their 340,715 total broke the 2001 New York Liberty record of 250,565 set over fewer games.

The effect lifted all boats. Every single WNBA team posted double-digit YoY attendance growth in 2024 — the first time in league history all 12 teams grew simultaneously. The league's lowest-growth teams (Phoenix +16.5%, Las Vegas +18.1%) still outperformed any typical season-over-season movement.

2024 still didn't match 2002. With 9,807 average fans per game, the WNBA's 2024 season ranked 3rd all-time — behind 1998 (10,864) and 1999 (10,205). The league's commercial infrastructure (media deals, arenas) is now significantly stronger, making the current trajectory potentially more durable than the late-90s peak.

Sellouts as a capacity signal. The jump from 45 to 154 sellouts (+242%) suggests that arena capacity — not fan demand — may now be the binding constraint for several teams. Washington, LA, and Atlanta relocated home games to larger NBA venues to accommodate demand, a telling operational signal for the league's expansion strategy.

Strategic Recommendations (Data-Driven)

Priority #1: Retain new fans, don't just count them. The 2024 growth was partly star-driven. The question for 2025 is what % of new Fever/Liberty fans re-engage. Teams should track renewal rates of new 2024 ticket buyers as the leading indicator of whether this is a structural shift or a temporary spike.

Capacity investment signals: Washington, LA, Atlanta. Three teams moved home games to larger NBA arenas to accommodate demand. This is unusually strong evidence that arena size is now constraining revenue — a strong business case for dedicated WNBA-first venue investment or permanent NBA-arena co-tenancy agreements.

The floor matters more than the ceiling. Atlanta Dream averaged just 3,006 fans in 2023 — the league floor. Even with double-digit growth, low-floor teams risk being stuck in a negative cycle of low attendance → low revenue → limited investment. League revenue sharing and targeted market development matter for the long-term competitive balance of the product.

Methodology & Data Sources

2023 per-team attendance: Sourced directly from Across the Timeline (acrossthetimeline.com), the primary public repository for WNBA attendance data, as compiled by kenn.com/blog/sports-attendance/all-time-wnba-attendance/. These figures match ESPN and Sports Reference records.

2024 league totals: Official WNBA press release (wnba.com/news/wnba-delivers-record-setting-2024-season, Sept. 27 2024). Total attendance = 2,353,735; average = 9,807; sellouts = 154; Indiana home total = 340,715.

2024 per-team estimates: Indiana Fever figures are exact (340,715 / 20 games = 17,036 avg). For other teams, percentage changes from the WNBA's official press release were used where published (Indiana +319%, LA Sparks +69%, NY Liberty +64%, Phoenix Mercury +16.5%, Las Vegas Aces +18.1%). Remaining teams were derived to be internally consistent with the official league total of 2,353,735. These figures should be treated as estimates, not official team-level data.

Historical data (1997–2023): Across the Timeline, sourcing team and league media guides.

Ticket revenue estimates: Directional only. $35 average ticket price is an industry estimate — the WNBA does not publish average ticket prices. This figure is used only to illustrate the scale of gate revenue change, not as a precise financial claim.
